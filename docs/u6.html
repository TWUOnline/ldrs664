Overview

To create authentic learning communities, we not only need authentic connections between students and faculty, but we also need authentic learning opportunities. In other words, we need students to engage deeply with what they are discussing, hearing, or reading in our classes.

In this unit, we will focus on how to create opportunities for authentic learning, including strategies to create community, give feedback, and nurture student success.

Unit 6 focuses on two guiding questions:

  • What teaching strategies can I use to create community within my courses?
  • How can I structure feedback to support learning and growth?

6.1 Building Community in Learning Environments

As we continue to consider how to create authentic learning communities, in this unit we will focus on strategies to build a deeper sense of community, as well as how to provide feedback to students to support their continued growth and development.

As facilitators of learning, we strive to create learning communities and experiences where students become deeply involved in their learning. In Small Teaching Online, Darby and Lang (2019), share stories of their own teaching, including strategies they have used to engage students in the learning process. These strategies go beyond the read-think-write formula we often see—particularly in online learning. Instead, the authors tell stories of deep engagement, including a story about create opportunities for students to think critically and communicate persuasively through the use of debate. As Darby and Lang (2019) write, students found these learning experiences valuable, sharing that “the debates we had in that class helped them to see others’ viewpoints, to consider perspectives that differed from their own – to expand their thinking” (p. 76).

For those learning online, of course, engagement will look different. When you facilitate learning in an online environment, you must be actively present in the discussions between students, supporting student learning by engaging their ideas and connecting with them personally. As Darby and Lang (2017) argue, “You set the tone and the example, both at the very beginning of the class and throughout the semester” (p. 86).

In online learning environments, students can often feel isolated—especially if the instructor has no visible presence. Darby and Lang (2017) use the analogy of a student showing up to an on campus class or to an employee training session, only to walk into an empty room with no visible instructor. Throughout the course, your presence matters. “If you students begin to feel that you are never in class, they’ll stop showing up, too, and the energy of the online learning community will take a nose-dive. It’s vitally important to be active in your class in order to help sustain the interactions in the class” (p. 87). This includes things like participating in online discussions, posting regular announcements (written or video), responding to student questions, and sharing a bit about who you are as a person.

6.2 Providing Feedback to Support Growth and Development

As facilitators, teachers, and leaders, we often need to provide feedback to others. Doing so can feel challenging—especially if we feel we must critique someone’s work or guide them to change something about what they are doing. And yet, teaching and facilitation, as well as leading, require that we provide this type of feedback and guidance to others.

Being able to provide feedback to learners is an essential component of effective facilitation and teaching. Thus, we must develop the skill of providing feedback that combines a mix of challenge and support. We can, at the same time, guide learners to high levels of achievement, while supporting their growth and development. We can, at the same time, point out the strengths of their work, as well as areas for growth.

Rethinking the Role of Feedback

To provide effective feedback, we need to consider our own positionality. If we enter the process of providing feedback as a judge who is evaluating a student’s achievement, we will always be able to find areas to critique—and our feedback can be perceived as critical and judgemental. When we are novice teachers, we often approach feedback this way, pointing out all the things that are wrong with a student’s work. However, this approach is not very effective. Even if accurate, by pointing out all the problems, we create a combative relationship in which we are the “expert” and the student is “failing.” (Consider, for a moment, how you have felt when teachers or leaders have approached you this way.)

Instead, when we approach feedback as an experience designed to support student learning, we can enter the experience understanding of course the student does not yet know everything about the topic or have the skills they need to complete the assignment without error. They are, after all, in this course or training precisely because they need to learn. Thus, it is our role as the facilitator to support their learning, providing guidance and feedback along the way to support their growth and learning.

Scaffolded Feedback

While we may still provide direct feedback by pointing out errors, we do so with a focus on helping them correct those errors in future assignments. This concept, referred to as scaffolding, allows students to enter into the learning process with the expectation that they will continue to grow throughout the course. Shute (2008) includes several examples of scaffolded feedback, including “models, cues, prompts, hints, practical solutions, and direct instruction” (p. 163). In your graduate courses, for example, an instructor might provide a model of an effective paper for an assignment or might give you direct instruction on how to correct your reference list to ensure it aligns with APA formatting guidelines. In other words, instead of simply saying something like “Good paper. Some problems with APA formatting,” an instructor might provide notes such as: “You’re making great progress on APA formatting. In your next paper, be sure to include the author’s last name and first initial. For example, you should format this article like this…” Formative feedback like this is affirmative and supportive, while at the same time directly correcting errors and providing guidance for how to get it correct the next time.

Formative Assessment

This feedback should be timely, of course. Feedback should also be what scholars of teaching and learning describe as “formative.” In other words—feedback should be tailored to the student’s learning, giving them guidance about what they should do as they move forward, to deepen their learning and expand their skills. “What students crave, and what too often they don’t receive, is feedback specifically designed to help them get better—especially through frequent, low-stakes assessments” (Darby & Lang, 2019, p. 11).

Peer Feedback

But feedback does not need to come only from the instructor; in a learning community, students can also provide feedback to each other—again, focusing on the growth and learning of their peers, not in judging their work. Online discussions allow this type of interaction to occur, as students are often asked to interact with the ideas of others, challenge their thinking, or ask questions to further their learning.

Goal-Directed Feedback

Shute (2008) argues that learner motivation depends on alignment between a student’s learning goals and their belief that they can achieve these goals. In other words, it is important that our feedback not only supports their continued motivation, but also moves them closer to achieving the learning they hope to achieve. “Goal-directed feedback provides learners with information about their progress toward a desired goal (or set of goals) rather than providing feedback on discrete responses” (Shute, 2008, p. 161).

Summary

In this unit, we have considered strategies we can use to build communities that support student learning, as well as how we can provide feedback that supports continued growth and development.

Building community not only supports learning—it is also an essential component of servant leadership. “Servant leaders build community to provide a place where people can feel safe and connected with others, but are still allowed to express their own individuality” (Northouse, 2022, p. 256). Within this type of community, learners can engage in their own learning—and contribute to the learning of others.

As a facilitator or leader, developing the skill of providing timely and effective feedback is important. With this skill set, you can create an environment in which those you teach or lead have the opportunity to grow and develop in their work. This focus on developing others is a key component of servant leadership.

Servant leaders are committed to helping each person in the organization grow personally and professionally. Commitment can take many forms, including providing the followers with opportunities for career development, helping them develop new work skills, taking a personal interest in their ideas, and involving them in decision-making” (Northouse, 2022, p. 256).
